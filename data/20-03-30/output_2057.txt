Then she found them new jobs  VANCOUVER -- On a Friday , Julie Pullen laid off a third of her staff at the hotel she manages in B .
's Interior .
By the Monday , she had most of them lined up with new jobs .
After letting the staff go , she just could n't sleep knowing she had sent seven people home without a source of income , the day after the provincial government declared a state of emergency due to COVID-19 .
" It was just heart wrenching , " said Pullen , general manager at Accent Inns in Kamloops .
So she opened her laptop and got to work .
She called around and found places that were in fact struggling to find workers .

Kamloops , which has a population of about 100,000 , has a healthy tourism industry and is a popular stopover for people driving between Vancouver and Calgary .
Yet , layoffs in seasonal industries such as tourism are not unusual .
Pullen , 43 , is no stranger to that part of the job .
" It 's easy when you can say , ' I 'll bring you back and we 'll hit the ground running , ' " she said .
But she knew it was different this time .
Rooms sat empty , and the breakfast room was quiet in the mornings .

Business at the hotel was down 50 per cent , Pullen said , and the novel coronavirus was showing no signs of slowing down .
As of Thursday morning , there were more than 659 confirmed cases of COVID-19 in the province , including 46 in B .
's Interior .
Pullen had taken the helm of the Accent Inn in Kamloops seven months ago .
Many of her employees had been working there for years but welcomed her with open arms , she said .

Staff morale at the hotel was down .
" When you look around and half your team is missing , it 's a little harder around here , " she said .

The entire hotel chain is offering a deal for health-care workers and other essential service personnel who are worried about taking the virus back to their families and instead want to self-isolate between shifts .
